# West Africa

Explored by the Europeans as early as the 15th and 16th centuries

17th and 18th centuries: slave trade

End of slave trade (first half of the 19th c.): colonisation

Colonial powers: Britain and France

Six West African countries give English the status of an/the official language

English is a lingua franca widely used in the region (especially its varieties, i.e., pidgin and creole, e.g., Krio)

West Africa

**Cameroon:** Formerly a German protectorate

- Divided between France and Britain in 1919
- Today both French and English are its official languages
- A number of indigenous languages

**The Gambia:** The smallest state in Africa

- English is the official language
- A number of African languages
- An English-based creole, Krio

# West Africa

**Ghana:** Independent state in 1957

- Former British colony of the Gold Coast and the former British Togoland trust territory
- English is the official language and the second language of at least 1,ooo,ooo Ghanians
- A large number of African languages (e.g. Akan, Hausa,...) are used as first languages by the population of Ghana

**Liberia:** Founded in 1847 as a home for freed American slaves (Americo-Liberians)

- The oldest republic in Africa; never a colony
- Cultural differences between the Americo-Liberians and the local African cultures
- The 198o coup
- English is the official language in Liberia; strong ties with US English

# West Africa

**Nigeria:** The most populous country in Africa; independence in 196o

- colonised by the British between 1861 and 19o6
- A single colony in 1914
- Over 4oo African languages are spoken in Nigeria
- English is the official language of the country
- A form of pidgin English is often used as a lingua franca

**Sierra Leone:** Founded by British abolitionists in late 18th century as a new home for freed slaves

- A British colony in early 19th century; independent in 1961
- English is the official language of the country
- West African English-based creole Krio used in all spheres of public life
- 1o% of the population are native speakers of Krio
- A number of African languages

# East Africa

Colonial powers: Britain, Germany and Portugal

English has an important role in six East African countries, all former British colonial territories

English is the/an official language, the language of politics, business, writing, the media, the medium of instruction

English is found especially in urban areas, and is understood by a minority of the population

Swahili or Kiswahili (a Bantu language) is widely used in the region as a _lingua franca_

East Africa

**Kenya:** British colony between 192o and 1963,

- A large number of different ethnic groups (over 6o indigenous East African languages)
- Two official languages: English and Swahili
- English is the language of higher education and the government and the language of international business, whereas Swahili is used as a lingua franca in all spheres of public life, and primary education

**Malawi:** Former British Nyasaland Protectorate

- Two official languages are English and Chewa (Nyanja)

# East Africa

**Tanzania:** Former British colonies of Tanganyka and Zanzibar

- The official languages of Tanzania are Swahili and English
- Swahili is used as the medium of instruction in primary education, while in higher education English is the medium of instruction
- Over 1oo East African languages are also used in the country

**Uganda:** British protectorate; an independent country in 1962

- Period of political unrest, civil war, human rights abuse and economic disaster
- The regime of Idi Amin in the 197os
- Today Uganda is relatively stable and successful in its fight against poverty
- A number of East African languages
- English is the official language, and Swahili is the _lingua franca_

# East Africa

**Zambia:** Northern Rhodesia

- English is the official language of the country
- A number of indigenous languages are spoken in Zambia

**Zimbabwe:** Southern Rhodesia

- Turbulent transition to independence
- A system of racial segregation in Rhodesia
- Civil war : independence in 198o
- Land redistribution scheme of 2ooo: accusations of human rights abuse
- English is the official language of the country
- Shona and Ndebele - national languages

# South Africa

- The southernmost country in Africa
- Three capital cities: Pretoria (executive), Cape Town (legislative) and Bloemfontein (judicial)

# Colonization of South Africa

- 1652 Dutch colonists reach Cape Town
- Gradual expansion of colonial territories
- British colonisation began during the Napoleonic Wars
- Britain seized control of the Cape Colony in 18o6
- Discovery of diamonds and gold (second half of the 19th c.)
- Numbers of European immigrants increased
- Ethnically mixed: native African peoples, Europeans, people from various Asian

nations (Malaysia, India,...)

# The Boers

- Afrikaans-speaking migrating farmers in eastern South Africa
- Attempted to gain independence from the British colonialists
- Defeated in the Second Anglo-Boer War (1899-19o2)
- Independent Boer Republics ceased to exist
- In 191o the _Union of South Africa_ was created (two former colonies, _Cape Colony_ and _Natal Colony_, and two former Boer Republics, _Transvaal_ and _Orange Free State_)

# Apartheid

- _Apartheid: separateness_ in Afrikaans
- A system of racial segregation introduced after WWII
- Races were officially separated in most spheres of life
- Political, legal and economic power was in the hands of the white minority
- _Apartheid_ came to an end with the first multi-racial elections in 1994
- 1994 Elections won by the African National Congress.
- Nine new provinces were created: Western Cape, Northern Cape, North-West,

Free State, Gauteng, Eastern Cape, Mpumalanga, Limpopo and Kwa-Zulu/Natal

Languages

11 official languages (Afrikaans, English, Ndebele, North Sotho, South

Sotho, Swazi, Tsonga, Tswana, Venda, Xhosa and Zulu)

Another 11 languages are officially promoted

English has always been a minority language in South Africa

Nevertheless, it dominates politics and business
