Canada

General information

Located in the northern part of the North American continent

Second largest country in the world

Six time zones

Relatively sparsely populated

Capital city: Ottawa

Federation

Two official languages, English and French

Climate

North: subarctic, tundra

Prairie provinces: continental - harsh winters, hot summers, relatively dry

South east: continental, humid

South, coastal areas: mild, rainy (especially the West Coast)

History

- First inhabitants: First Nations peoples and the Inuit
- 1000 AD the Vikings
- 1497 John Cabot
- 1534 Jacques Cartier explored and mapped the Canadian shores on behalf of King

Francis I of France

- 1605 and 1609 First permanent European settlements in Canada, Port Royal and Quebec City
- First British colonies established in Newfoundland

# History

- 17th and 18th centuries: French and Indian Wars (Intercolonial Wars)
- 1763 Treaty of Paris: Britain gains control of Canada
- 1783 Treaty of Paris: Recognition of an independent USA (loyalists)
- 19th c. Expansion of British colonies (west, north)
- 1867: Constitution Act: Canada becomes a dominion (Ontario, Quebec, Nova Scotia, New Brunswick)
- New provinces and territories join the self-governing colony

# Language

- Two official languages: English, French
- Equal status in Parliament, federal courts, and all federal institutions
- Official-language minorities are guaranteed their own schools in all provinces and

territories

- English: 1st language of 60% of the population
- French: 1st language of 24% of the population
- Francophone population: 85% live in Quebec

# Language

- Status of the two languages varies, depending on the province: some have no official language, Quebec only has French as the official language, New Brunswick is officially bilingual, etc.
- Northwest territories: several indigenous languages have official status
- Nunavut: three official languages, including Inuktitut, the language of the majority of the population
- Other important languages: Chinese, Italian, German

# Provinces and Territories

Federation

- Ten provinces
- Three territories

The powers of the provinces are greater than the powers of the

territories.

Newfoundland and Labrador

- Capital: St. John‘s
- The easternmost province of Canada
- Comprises two parts: the island of Newfoundland and the Labrador Peninsula
- Climate ranges from polar to subarctic

# Nova Scotia

- Capital: Halifax
- Small province located in southeastern Canada
- One of the Maritime provinces
- Traditionally: fishing a major economic activity
- Recent decades: other sectors (natural resources, small businesses, tourism)

# Prince Edward Island

- Capital: Charlottetown
- The smallest province in Canada
- One of the Maritime provinces
- Its economy relies on agriculture, fishing and tourism

# New Brunswick

- Capital: Fredericton
- One of the Maritime provinces
- Continental climate
- Majority of the population is English speaking
- A significant French-speaking minority (approximately 35% of the population)

# Quebec

- Capital: Quebec City
- Located in central Canada
- The largest province in the country
- The only Canadian province with French as the only official language
- The largest city in the province is Montreal, the second largest city in Canada

# Ontario

- Capital: Toronto
- South central Canada
- Second largest province in the country in terms of land area
- Largest in terms of population
- Ottawa is located in Ontario
- Toronto: the largest city in the country and the heart of the country's financial sector

# Manitoba

- Capital: Winnipeg
- The easternmost of Canada's three Prairie Provinces
- Southern part: agriculture
- Northern part: covered by forests

# Saskatchewan

- Capital: Regina
- One of the Prairie Provinces
- Located east of Alberta and west of Manitoba
- Its climate ranges from subarctic in the north to continental in the south
- One Canada's agricultural provinces

# Alberta

- Capital: Edmonton
- The westernmost of Canada's three Prairie Provinces
- Its western border with British Columbia is formed by the peaks of the Rocky

Mountains

- Rich in timber and natural resources such as oil, gas and coal

# British Columbia

- Capital: Victoria
- The westernmost province of Canada
- Located on the Pacific Coast
- Economic growth and opportunities
- Vancouver is Canada's largest port on its West Coast

# Nunavut

- Capital: Iqaluit
- Established in 1999, formerly part of the Northwest territories
- Very large: northern Canada and the Arctic Archipelago
- Sparsely populated (Inuit, First Nations)

# Northwest Territories

- Capital: Yellowknife
- Located in northern Canada, east of Yukon and west of Nunavut
- Sparsely populated (First Nations)
- Rich in natural resources (gold, diamonds, petroleum, natural gas)

# Yukon Territory

- Capital: Whitehorse
- Located in northern Canada
- Westernmost territory (east of Alaska)
- Sparsely populated (First Nations)
- Fur trading, gold rushes
- Mining, tourism (outdoors)
