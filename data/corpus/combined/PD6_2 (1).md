# The Caribbean

- Cultural ties with coastal areas of Central and South America, bordering the Caribbean Sea, including 2 former British colonies, Belize (Central America) and Guyana (South America)
- Cultural ties with the Bahamas (not actually located in the Caribbean Sea)

History: Columbus and the Spanish

- - Christopher Columbus
    - Amerindian tribes: the Arawak, the Caribs and the Ciboney
    - Columbus referred to the natives as “Indians”
    - “West Indies” (sometimes used for the Caribbean Islands)
    - Spanish colonists claimed the territories they reached for the

Spanish monarchy

## History: Amerindian tribes

- The colonists believed it was their right to enslave and subdue the natives
- Most of the native tribes of the Caribbean disappeared along with the cultures and languages (slavery, wars, diseases, above all, smallpox)
- Words from the indigenous languages were imported into European languages

(canoe, tobacco, cannibal, hurricane, etc.)

## History: Plantation economy

- Main crops: tobacco, cotton and sugar
- A labour force was needed
- West African slaves: brought to the Caribbean to work on the plantations owned

by wealthy Europeans

## History: Slave trade

- First half of the 19th century: slavery in the Caribbean was officially abolished (1834 in the British colonies)
- Indentured workers from Asia
- Multicultural and ethnically mixed population

## The Caribbean today

- Very little of the profit was invested into the colonies
- Agriculture
- Main crops: sugar, tobacco and bananas
- Tourism: the most important source of income for many of the islands
- Often affected by hurricanes (devastate the crops and destroy the infrastructure)

## Language

- English is used in many of the islands of the Caribbean Sea
- Standard English is used as the official language or one of the official languages
- New varieties of English (Creole)

## Language

- Result of contact between the colonial languages (English, French, Spanish, Dutch), the local Amerindian languages and the languages of African slaves and Asian immigrants
- In some areas, English-based Creoles are widely used, but another language (e.g.,

Spanish) is the official language

## Pidgin

A system of communication, not a native language

Emerged among people who do not share a common language

Need to communicate (usually for reasons of trade)

Typically involves a limited vocabulary, a reduced grammatical structure, and a narrow range of functions (compared to the languages from which it derives)

Creole

Spanish _criollo_

- - People born in the colonies (lower class; European-born immigrants: upper class)

A pidgin language which has become the mother tongue of a community

Found in the Caribbean, West Africa, Pacific Ocean
