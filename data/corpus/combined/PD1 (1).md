Colony

- An area politically controlled by a certain state/country
- "The mother country" (colonizing nation)
- Negative connotation (exploitation)
- Dependencies, dependent territories, overseas territories

# Related terms

- Protectorate: formally autonomous area under political or military protection of a certain state
- Mandate: former colonies of the countries defeated in WWI, placed under protection of the Allied Powers by the League of Nations after WWI
- Dominion: above all the Inner Circle Nations

# Colonialism

- The process of extending the sovereignty beyond the borders of a state
- Control of the resources, people, economy, society
- Political and economic intervention
- Socio-cultural intervention
- Ideology

# Post-colonialism

- Societies and literatures of the former colonies
- Edward Said
- Close ties with postmodern literature and philosophy
- x Neocolonialism!

# Commonwealth

- Common well-being
- Commonwealth of Nations
- Voluntary association of independent states
- Former UK colonies, UK itself and a few other nations
- None of the states have control over the other states
- Symbolic head: British Monarch
- An international association
- The successor of the British Empire

# Anglophone /Anglosphere

- English-speaking
- Not a clearly defined concept
- Not all the Anglosphere nations are entirely Anglophone (Francophone regions in

Canada)

# Kachru’s model

- Anglosphere:
- The Inner Circle
- The Outer Circle
- The Expanding Circle

Kachru, B. B. (1985) "Standards, codifications and sociolinguistic realism: The English language in the outer circle." In R. Quirk and H.C.G. Widdowson (eds.). _English in the World: Teaching and Learning the Language and Literatures._ Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 11-3o.

The Inner Circle

English is the first language (L1) of the majority of the population

Australia, Canada, Ireland, New Zealand, USA, UK

The Outer Circle

English is taught as the second language (L2) – ESL

English may have a special status (e.g., it is one of the official languages, the language of higher education,...)

Former colonies (e.g., Singapore, India, Malawi,... )

The Expanding Circle

English is taught as a foreign language - EFL

Not former colonies

International communication (e.g., China, Slovenia, Greece,...)

The spread of English

- British Isles (end of 12th c.)
- The Americas and South Asia (17th c.)
- Australia, New Zealand, Southeast Asia and the South Pacific (18th c.)
- Africa (19th c.)

# Beginnings

- End of 12 c.: three Celtic-speaking areas, Ireland, Wales, and Scotland
- Expansion beyond the British Isles began in the early 17th century

# 17th century: the Americas

- 1584, the lost colony of Roanoke
- 16o7, Jamestown
- 162o, the Mayflower, Massachusetts
- First British colonies in Canada in Newfoundland
- By 1763 Britain seizes control of the French colonies in Canada
- 1623, first British Caribbean colony, Saint Kitts,
- Other islands (some seized from the French and the Spanish)
- A few colonies in Central and South America

# 17th century: South Asia

- Involvement in South Asia began in 16oo
- The British East India Company was granted a royal charter giving it a monopoly in trade
- The company gained political influence and effectively ruled the Indian

Subcontinent

- British Empire formally seized control of the area in 1857

# 18th century: Australia

- 177o, James Cook explores the east Australian coast and claimed it for Britain
- 1788, British colonisation of Australia begins (penal colony in New South Wales)
- Expansion of British possessions
- 1868, transportation of British convicts to Australia ends

# 18th century: New Zealand

- 1769-7o, James Cook circumnavigated and mapped the two main islands of the New Zealand archipelago
- Late 18th century, first European settlers began to arrive
- 184o, the Treaty of Waitangi

18th century: Southeast Asia and South Pacific

- Expeditions of various explorers, most notably James Cook
- Late 18th century, the British began to exert their influence in certain areas of the Southeast Asia and the South Pacific

# 19th century: Africa

- End of the 18th century, first British possession on the African continent: the Cape Colony, a former Dutch colony
- Gained by the UK during the Napoleonic wars
- The race for Africa began
- The beginning of World War I, almost the whole continent was divided among the European colonial powers
- After World War II: a repartitioning of the continent
- Independent African countries emerged in the 196os and 197os
