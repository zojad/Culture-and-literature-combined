**SOCIETIES AND CULTURES OF THE ENGLISH- SPEAKING WORLD**

New Zealand

General information

Located in the Southern Hemisphere in the south-west Pacific Ocean

Comprises two main islands and a number of smaller islands

Similar in size to Japan or the British Isles

Active volcanoes and frequent earthquakes

Temperate climate

The South Island

- The larger of the two main islands
- Southern Alps: a mountain range running along the entire length of the island
- Temperate climate, cooler
- Christchurch – largest city of the South Island

The North Island

- The more populous of the two main islands (approximately ¾ of the population)
- Volcanic activity (Mount Ruapehu)
- Temperate climate (warmer)
- Auckland - largest city
- Wellington - the nation’s capital

Biodiversity

- Isolation of the islands
- Many endemic species
- No native nonmarine mammals (except for 3 bat species, now extinct)
- Reptiles (tuatara)
- Birds: kakapo, moa (extinct) and kiwi, seabirds
- Fish
- Insects
- Human impact

History

- Eastern Polynesians: Maori
- 1642 Dutch explorer Abel Tasman
- 1768–71 British explorer James Cook mapped New Zealand
- European and North American whalers, sealers, traders, Christian missionaries
- Lawlessness, interest of other European nations: British government claims sovereignty
- 1840 Treaty of Waitangi: Differences in the Maori in English versions

History

- 1841 New Zealand becomes a colony (before that part of New South Wales)
- 1856 The colony became effectively self-governing
- 1907 New Zealand became an independent dominion
- 1931 Statute of Westminster
- 1947 The Statute is adopted; New Zealand becomes an independent nation

People and culture

5 million people

Almost ¼ of the population: born outside New Zealand

Majority of European descent (mainly of British or Irish descent)

Strong ties to British culture

Immigration

Approximately 15%: Maori

others: of Asian descent, of Pacific Island origin

Maori

- Indigenous population: Polynesian people, Maori or Māori
- Aotearoa
- Reached New Zealand between the 9th and the 14th century AD
- Rich cultural and religious tradition
- Arts (wood carving, weaving, performing – dance, music - singing)
- Language: close to Eastern Polynesian (Tahiti)

Maori

- Contact with the Europeans affected the Maori communities: intertribal conflicts, diseases
- 1840: Treaty of Waitangi - some (but not all) Maori chiefs ceded Maori sovereignty to UK
- As the European settlement grew, Maori culture declined
- The Maori became a minority in the second half of the 19th century

Maori

- Marginalisation, assimilation
- Communal land was taken from the tribal communities
- A political and cultural revival
- Compensation claims
- 1987: Maori Language Act

Language

- New Zealand has three official languages: English, Maori and New Zealand Sign Language
- Bilingual names of official institutions
- Maori is rarely used in dealings with official institutions
- Television channels, schools
- Community language in a small number of settlements
